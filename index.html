Hidden Water Rules You Should Know

The federal government regulates water using these specific, non-obvious guidelines:

  1. The Cost vs. Health Gap (MCL vs. MCLG): The EPA sets a Maximum Contaminant Level Goal (MCLG) where health risks are exactly zero. However, utilities are not legally forced to meet it. Instead, they must follow the Maximum Contaminant Level (MCL), which allows trace chemical amounts if removing them completely is too expensive for the city.
  2. The 10-Year Lead Clock (LCRI Rule): Under the federal Lead and Copper Rule Improvements, public water utilities are legally mandated to identify and completely replace 100% of lead service lines within 10 years. Your city is required to provide a public map of these lines.
  3. The Forgotten Annual Report (CCR): By law, your water provider must publish a comprehensive Consumer Confidence Report by July 1st every single year. They rarely mail them directly to homes anymore; you must look them up online manually.

The Contaminant Encyclopedia

Filter the list below to understand common water components, their legal limits, and when you should worry. Click any title to expand the details:


PFAS / PFOS

What it is: Forever chemicals from industrial waste, non-stick products, and waterproofing sprays that accumulate over time.

The Legal Limit (MCL): 4.0 parts per trillion (ppt) for PFOA and PFOS.

When to worry: If your local public water system updates show persistent chemical metrics running above the 4.0 ppt marker across multiple consecutive quarters.

Lead

What it is: A highly toxic heavy metal that leaches directly into drinking water from aging household plumbing fixtures or underground lead service lines.

The Legal Limit (Action Level): 15 parts per billion (ppb).

When to worry: If you live in a building constructed before 1986 and routinely drink water that has sat completely stagnant in the pipes for more than 6 hours.

Copper

What it is: A metallic element that dissolves into water from corroding residential copper pipes, often leaving blue-green stains on sinks.

The Legal Limit (Action Level): 1.3 parts per million (ppm).

When to worry: If your water tastes noticeably metallic, causes stomach distress, or if your city fails its regional plumbing monitoring thresholds.

Cyanotoxins

What it is: Dangerous poisons produced by blue-green harmful algal blooms (HABs) in warm lakes, reservoirs, or slow-moving rivers during summer.

The Legal Limit: No permanent federal MCL, but states deploy immediate health advisories using EPA safety thresholds.

When to worry: Only during sudden, severe summer heat waves if local health officials issue an explicit, temporary Do Not Drink alert.

Nitrates

What it is: Chemical compounds originating from agricultural fertilizers, commercial manure runoff, and failing backyard septic tanks.

The Legal Limit (MCL): 10 milligrams per liter (mg/L).

When to worry: If you drink from a shallow private well located directly adjacent to active farmland or commercial livestock operations.

Arsenic

What it is: A highly toxic, naturally occurring element found in regional rock formations that can erode into underground aquifers.

The Legal Limit (MCL): 0.010 mg/L (or 10 parts per billion).

When to worry: If you rely on unmonitored private well water in geologically high-arsenic territories without a reverse osmosis filtration system.

Coliforms and E. coli

What it is: Bacterial organisms used by scientists as indicators that sewage, animal feces, or surface pathogens may have breached the water network.

The Legal Limit: No more than 5% of monthly system samples can test positive for total coliforms. Any single E. coli positive sample triggers an emergency.

When to worry: If a water main break or system failure prompts local utilities to issue an immediate, mandatory Boil Water Advisory.

Where to Audit Your Local Water System

Use these free, verified tools to cross-reference your specific location with national data:

  • EPA Consumer Confidence Report Finder – Search your zip code to read your city's official water testing scores.
  • EWG Tap Water Database – Review independent health-focused diagnostic metrics by utility.
